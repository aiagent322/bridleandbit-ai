About the WPRA

The Women's Professional Rodeo Association didn't start as a polished institution — it started as an act of defiance. In 1948, a group of women ranchers and riders — many of whom had been running family operations alone while husbands, fathers, and brothers served in World War II — gathered in San Angelo, Texas and formed the Girls Rodeo Association (GRA). These were women whose riding and roping skills had been forged on the open ranges of the American West, and they had grown tired of being reduced to beauty pageants and cigarette case prizes while men competed for real money in the same arenas.

Today the WPRA is headquartered in Colorado Springs, Colorado, with over 3,000 members from the US, Canada, and Australia. It is the oldest women's sports association in the country and the only professional sports association governed entirely by women. Members compete for more than $5 million in total prize money annually at PRCA/WPRA-sanctioned rodeos across the continent.

"I remember having an All-Girl rodeo in Amarillo, Texas, in late 1947 and then we all decided to meet in San Angelo in 1948 and the GRA was formed."

— Dixie Reger Mosley, WPRA Founding Member

A History of Fighting for Equality

1948
Girls Rodeo Association founded in San Angelo, Texas. First year pays out $29,000 to contestants. Founding members include women ranchers who ran operations solo during WWII.
1955
GRA President Jackie Worthington and RCA President Bill Linderman sign landmark agreement — the RCA will strongly urge rodeo committees to use GRA-approved barrel races and will not contract with any competing association.
1967
Barrel racing joins the NFR — the sport's biggest stage. Women finally compete for world championship honors at the same event as their male counterparts.
1974
First WPRA World Champion Breakaway Roper crowned, establishing the event's championship legacy even before it reached the mainstream PRCA circuit.
1981
Name officially changed from Girls Rodeo Association to the Women's Professional Rodeo Association — reflecting the maturity and professionalism of the organization.
1985
Equal prize money achieved at PRCA rodeos — making the WPRA the first professional women's sports organization in the country to achieve fiscal equality with male counterparts. A landmark in women's sports history.
2017
Breakaway roping introduced at PRCA-sanctioned rodeos for the first time — starting in the Columbia River Circuit with events in Ellensburg and Pendleton. A new era begins.
2025
Breakaway roping contested at 560 PRCA rodeos — up from just 30 in 2019. One of the fastest growth stories in professional rodeo history.

Primary Events

Barrel Racing
WPRA Flagship Event · 700+ Annual Rodeos
Contestants on horseback run a cloverleaf pattern around three barrels set in a triangle pattern in the arena. Scored on time — hundredths of a second separate world-class runs from the rest. The World Champion Barrel Racer is determined by money won at WPRA-approved barrel races across the season, with the top 15 money earners qualifying for the NFR. Members may enter up to 100 official events per season. Barrel racing has been part of the NFR since 1967.
NFR since 1967 · 700+ PRCA rodeos
Breakaway Roping
Fastest-Growing Event · 560 PRCA Rodeos in 2025
A roper on horseback lassos a calf — the rope is tied lightly to the saddle horn with a breakaway string that snaps free when the calf hits the end of the rope. Scored on time. The event requires precision, speed, and exceptional horsemanship. First contested at PRCA rodeos in 2017 in just the Columbia River Circuit; by 2025 it was in 560 PRCA rodeos. Members may enter up to 80 official events per season.

Championship Events

The WPRA's two championship events crown the best barrel racers and breakaway ropers in the world each December — one in Las Vegas alongside the PRCA's NFR, and one as a standalone championship that has grown into one of the sport's most anticipated finales.

Wrangler NFR — Barrel Racing
December · Thomas & Mack Center, Las Vegas, NV
The top 15 barrel racers in the world standings compete over 10 rounds at the NFR alongside the PRCA's biggest stars. The 2025 World Champion Barrel Racer was Hailey Kinsel, whose horse DM Sissy Hayday was also named AQHA Nutrena Barrel Racing Horse of the Year. Carlee Otero was a standout performer at the 2025 event, posting the fastest time of the entire NFR in Round 9 and winning Round 5.
2025 Champion: Hailey Kinsel
Wrangler National Finals Breakaway Roping
December · Top 15 breakaway ropers · 10 rounds
A standalone championship for the top 15 breakaway ropers in the world standings competing over 10 rounds. The 2025 NFBR was relocated from its originally scheduled venue at the South Point Arena in Las Vegas to Cowtown Coliseum in Fort Worth, Texas (December 22–23) due to EHV biosecurity concerns — marking the first time the NFBR was held in Texas since the inaugural 2020 event. Munsell won her first world title. Shelby George won the Average title.
2025 Champion: Munsell · Average: Shelby George

All-Women's Division

Beyond the PRCA partnership, the WPRA sanctions its own All-Women's Division — rodeos held exclusively for female competitors. These events feature four events: breakaway calf roping, tie-down calf roping, team roping, and barrel racing. Contestants accumulate points throughout the season to qualify for the WPRA World Finals, held at the Extraco Events Center in Waco, Texas (since 2014). It is the competitive home for women who compete exclusively on the all-women's circuit rather than the PRCA mainstream schedule.
