About USDA & APHIS

The United States Department of Agriculture touches the horse industry in more ways than most horse owners realize. Through its Animal and Plant Health Inspection Service (APHIS) — an $800 million agency employing roughly 7,000 people across ports, borders, and farms — the USDA administers the laws and programs that govern equine health monitoring, disease surveillance, import quarantine, the Horse Protection Act, and biosecurity funding that reaches every corner of the industry.

APHIS was formally established in 1972, though its roots trace to 1854 when the first federal agricultural inspection functions were created. It is the primary federal authority for protecting US agriculture from invasive pests and diseases. Its Deputy Administrator for Veterinary Services serves as the Chief Veterinary Officer of the United States — and represents the US at the World Organisation for Animal Health (OIE). Every Coggins test, every horse imported from Europe, every EHV outbreak response — all run through the federal framework APHIS maintains.

"These projects will provide producers, veterinarians, and states with tools to better control, treat, and recover from foreign animal disease outbreaks."

— Michael Watson, APHIS Administrator, on the 2025 NADPRP disease prevention grants

Key Programs for the Horse Industry

Horse Protection Act
HPA · Enforced by APHIS VMOs
Prohibits the showing, exhibiting, selling, or transporting of "sored" horses — animals subjected to painful techniques on their front feet to exaggerate their gait, most associated with the Tennessee Walking Horse industry. APHIS Veterinary Medical Officers conduct inspections at covered events. A sweeping regulatory overhaul published May 2024 is replacing the industry's Designated Qualified Person system with APHIS-trained Horse Protection Inspectors — effective December 31, 2026.
HPI System effective Dec 31, 2026
National Animal Health Monitoring System
NAHMS · Equine Health Studies
APHIS's statistical unit responsible for national equine population estimates and health management studies. NAHMS conducted comprehensive equine studies in 1998, 2005, and 2015 — each a landmark in understanding the health, demographics, and management practices of US horse operations. The next study, NAHMS Equine 2026, is underway with Phase I contacting operations in all 50 states with five or more equids beginning August 2026.
NAHMS Equine 2026 — Phase I Aug 2026
Disease Surveillance & Import Quarantine
APHIS Veterinary Services
APHIS Veterinary Services oversees national equine disease surveillance, outbreak investigations, and all import quarantine for horses entering the US. Horses requiring quarantine are held in USDA-APHIS facilities or APHIS-approved private quarantine facilities, where they are examined and tested for foreign animal diseases. APHIS also responds to disease emergencies — including the EHV outbreaks that disrupted multiple major horse events in 2025.
Coggins · Import quarantine · EHV response
National Agricultural Statistics Service Census
NASS · Every 5 Years
The USDA's NASS conducts a Census of Agriculture every five years, counting equine populations on registered farms nationwide. This is the federal government's primary equine population data source — though it underestimates total US horse numbers since it excludes horses at boarding facilities, racetracks, and other non-farm operations, which can account for more than half the actual national herd.

Horse Protection Act — 2025/2026 Update

A major regulatory overhaul of the Horse Protection Act was published by APHIS in May 2024. The rule replaces the industry's Designated Qualified Person (DQP) system with APHIS-trained Horse Protection Inspectors (HPIs) — who must be licensed veterinarians, vet techs, or state/local animal welfare officers. The HPI training program is currently paused for curriculum reevaluation. The new system takes full effect December 31, 2026. Until then, existing DQP regulations remain in force. For 2025, APHIS is exercising enforcement discretion on the scar rule consistent with practice from 2017–2023.

Why It Matters to the Horse Industry

Every horse transported across state lines, every international import, every disease outbreak response, and every major biosecurity protocol in the US runs through USDA APHIS in some form. When equine herpesvirus outbreaks forced venue changes at the NRCHA Snaffle Bit Futurity and the NFBR in late 2025, it was USDA biosecurity guidelines and state animal health officials working under the federal APHIS framework that shaped each decision — from whether events could proceed to which biosecurity measures were required.

When you get a Coggins test before hauling your horse, the equine infectious anemia testing infrastructure runs through APHIS-approved laboratories. When a horse comes off a plane from Europe, a USDA import quarantine facility is its first stop. When a new disease threat emerges — like the New World Screwworm concern in 2025 — it is APHIS that coordinates the federal response, funds the research, and issues the emergency guidance that flows down to veterinarians, producers, and horse owners across the country.

The USDA is not a membership organization, a competition body, or an advocacy group. It is the quiet infrastructure that keeps the American horse industry operating safely — and its decisions directly shape what is and isn't possible at every level of equine competition, commerce, and care.
