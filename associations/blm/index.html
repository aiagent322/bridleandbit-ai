About the BLM Wild Horse & Burro Program

The Bureau of Land Management is a federal agency within the US Department of the Interior, managing approximately 245 million acres of public land — primarily in 12 western states — on behalf of the American people. For the horse industry, the BLM's most consequential role is its mandate under the Wild Free-Roaming Horses and Burros Act of 1971 to protect and manage wild horses and burros as part of a thriving natural ecological balance on public lands.

That mandate is one of the most complex balancing acts in American land management. Wild horse and burro herds have virtually no natural predators, and their populations can double approximately every four years. The BLM must weigh the protection of these iconic animals — symbols of the American West enshrined in federal law — against the ecological health of the rangelands they share with livestock, wildlife, and other users. It is a mission that draws passionate advocates on every side, and shapes the lives of tens of thousands of horses across the West.

"Each horse adopted is a life changed, and every adopter becomes part of the story of wild horse conservation."

— Melissa Foster, BLM Montana/Dakotas Wild Horse & Burro Specialist

Population & the Numbers Problem

The BLM manages wild horses and burros in 175 Herd Management Areas (HMAs) across 10 western states, covering approximately 27 million acres of public land. For each HMA, the BLM sets an Appropriate Management Level (AML) — the number of animals the land can sustainably support in balance with other resources and uses. The current national AML upper limit is 25,556 animals.

The challenge is stark. As of March 1, 2025, the estimated on-range population was 73,130 wild horses and burros — nearly three times the AML. An additional 64,205 animals (as of August 2025) are housed in off-range holding facilities maintained by the BLM, bringing the total population under federal care to well over 137,000. The FY2025 congressional appropriation for the wild horse and burro program was $142 million — nearly 600% higher in nominal dollars than the $20.4 million appropriated in FY2000.

The 10 Western States

BLM Herd Management Areas span 10 western states. Nevada dominates with 83 of the nation's 175 HMAs — nearly half the national total. Wyoming, Oregon, Utah, and California each have between 14 and 21 HMAs.

Adoption Program

Since 1971, the BLM has placed more than 290,000 wild horses and burros into private care through adoptions and sales. Adoption remains one of the agency's most important tools for managing excess populations. Adopters can attend in-person events across the country, visit a BLM facility directly, or bid through the Online Corral — the BLM's online adoption platform. The standard adoption fee is $125. Animals are wild and untrained unless otherwise noted.

In-Person Adoption Events
The BLM holds adoption events across the country throughout the year, often featuring horsemanship clinics alongside the animals available for adoption. A May 2025 event in Hamilton, Montana placed 26 of 36 offered horses — a 72% placement rate — with a high bid of $350 for a dun gelding from Nevada. Events regularly feature yearlings, adults, and trained animals.
$125 adoption fee · Events nationwide
Online Corral
The BLM's online bidding platform lets potential adopters browse available wild horses and burros, compete in timed auctions, and select a pickup location near them. The Online Corral has dramatically expanded the geographic reach of the adoption program, connecting eastern adopters with western mustangs. Adopters complete their application online and must be pre-approved before bidding.
Competitive online bidding · Nationwide pickup
BLM Off-Range Facilities
A network of BLM-operated off-range corrals and long-term pastures houses animals that have been removed from overpopulated ranges. Many facilities have regular business hours and accept adoption inquiries by appointment. As of August 2025, 64,205 animals are housed off-range — 61% in long-term pastures, 39% in short-term corrals being prepared for adoption or sale.
64,205 animals off-range (Aug 2025)
Adoption Incentive Program — Ended
The AIP previously offered up to $1,000 to adopters of untrained wild horses and burros to help offset care costs. A federal court order issued March 3, 2025 enjoined further implementation. The BLM stopped issuing incentive payments for animals not titled by March 3, 2025. Standard adoptions continue unaffected — only the cash incentive has ended. The BLM is assessing next steps.

Gathers, Removals & Fertility Control

When herd populations exceed AML, the BLM conducts aerial gathers — using helicopters to round up excess animals from public rangelands. Removed animals are examined by a veterinarian, treated for any health issues, and prepared for adoption or transferred to off-range holding facilities. In FY2022, over 20,193 animals were removed from the range. The BLM's stated goal is to remove approximately 20,000 animals annually while placing 10,000+ into private care through adoptions and sales.

The BLM also uses fertility control vaccines — primarily PZP — to slow herd growth and reduce the long-term need for gathers. In FY2022, the agency treated a record 1,622 mares with contraceptives, approximately 4% of all wild mares. Each treatment costs roughly $2,500, including gather, treat, and short-term holding costs. The BLM is actively researching longer-lasting or permanent birth control options. Volunteers play a critical role in fertility control programs where horses are approachable — darting mares with vaccines in the field rather than requiring a full gather.

The BLM also launched a new tool in late 2025 — "Where did my horse come from?" — allowing adopters, trainers, and owners to trace the history and origin of their specific wild horse or burro using BLM records. In honor of America's 250th anniversary in 2026, the BLM launched a national tour featuring Freedom (a wild horse) and Liberty (a wild burro) to celebrate the landscapes and heritage of the American West.

The 1971 Act & Legal Framework

The Wild Free-Roaming Horses and Burros Act of 1971 is the foundation of the entire program. Signed into law after a national groundswell of public concern over the decline of wild horse populations — which had fallen from an estimated million-plus animals in the late 1800s to roughly 17,000 by the early 1970s — the Act directs the BLM to manage wild horses and burros as an integral part of the natural system of public lands. It prohibits the BLM from relocating herds to areas where they did not exist prior to the Act's passage, and for decades Congress has prohibited funds for sales of wild horses that result in slaughter or commercial processing.

The dual mandate the Act creates — protect the animals and protect the land — is the source of ongoing tension and litigation. Advocacy groups regularly challenge BLM gather operations and management decisions in federal court. The March 2025 court order that ended the Adoption Incentive Program is one recent example of how legal action directly shapes the program's operations.
