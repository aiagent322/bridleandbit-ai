About the PRCA

It started with a strike. In 1936, approximately 35 cowboys walked out of a rodeo at Boston Garden to protest promoter W.T. Johnson's refusal to add their entry fees to the total purse. Johnson eventually relented, and that successful protest gave birth to the Cowboys' Turtle Association — named because while they were slow to organize, they weren't afraid to stick their necks out when it mattered. The organization became the Rodeo Cowboys Association in 1945, and adopted its current name in 1975 to reflect its professional standards and growth.

Today the PRCA is headquartered in Colorado Springs, Colorado, and stands as the oldest and largest professional rodeo sanctioning body in the world. With over 6,000 members — including more than 5,200 contestants from the US, Canada, Mexico, and beyond — the PRCA sanctions more than 700 rodeos per year, distributing over $65 million in prize money annually. More than 43 million Americans identify as ProRodeo fans, making rodeo seventh in overall attendance among major US sporting events — ahead of golf and tennis.

Unlike most professional sports, PRCA cowboys are not paid salaries. They pay to enter each rodeo out of their own pocket. If they place, they profit. If they don't, they absorb the entry fee and every travel expense. Every run is a personal gamble — skill and grit against risk and injury — which is precisely what has defined rodeo since its earliest days.

"The name was chosen because, while they were slow to organize, when required they were unafraid to stick out their necks to get what they wanted — like turtles might do."

— On the founding of the Cowboys' Turtle Association, 1936

Sanctioned Events

The PRCA sanctions seven core events at the National Finals Rodeo, with women's barrel racing and breakaway roping contested through its longstanding partnership with the Women's Professional Rodeo Association (WPRA). The All-Around World Championship — the most prestigious individual honor in professional rodeo — goes to the cowboy who earns the most money across two or more events during the season.

Wrangler National Finals Rodeo

The Super Bowl of Rodeo
Wrangler NFR — Las Vegas, Nevada

The NFR is the PRCA's championship event and the richest rodeo in the world. Held each December at the Thomas & Mack Center on the UNLV campus in Las Vegas, Nevada, the NFR brings together the top 15 money earners in each event for 10 consecutive nights of competition. World championships are determined by total season earnings, including NFR prize money — meaning the title isn't settled until the final night.

The NFR has been held in Las Vegas since 1985. In June 2024, the PRCA and Las Vegas Events signed an extension keeping the NFR in Las Vegas through 2035, a deal valued at $264 million in total prize money for contestants and stock contractors. Every world champion receives a gold buckle and a specially crafted trophy saddle. The event is broadcast live on The Cowboy Channel and streamed on the Cowboy+ app, with Cowboy Christmas — a massive western gift show — held concurrently at the Las Vegas Convention Center.

12 Regional Circuits

The PRCA operates 12 US regional circuits, each with its own circuit finals. Circuit season champions and circuit finals average champions qualify for the NFR Open — held each July at the Pikes Peak or Bust Rodeo in Colorado Springs, Colorado — where regional champions compete for national circuit titles. The PRCA also has partnerships with the Canadian Professional Rodeo Association (CPRA's Maple Leaf Circuit) and the Federación Mexicana de Rodeo (PRCA-FMR Tour).

ProRodeo Hall of Fame

Located in Colorado Springs, the ProRodeo Hall of Fame inducts new members annually — honoring both athletes and livestock. Entire rodeos have earned induction, including the Guymon Pioneer Days Rodeo in Oklahoma, the only Oklahoma rodeo to receive the honor. The Hall tracks the sport's history from its 1936 founding through the modern era, preserving the legacy of every world champion, legendary bucking horse and bull, and stock contractor who helped build professional rodeo into what it is today.
